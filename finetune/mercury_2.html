Mercury 2 on transcript formatting

Does a diffusion language model beat fine-tuned 9B transformers?

Inception Labs' Mercury 2 is the first reasoning-capable diffusion language model. We ran it on the same held-out transcript-formatting test set we used for the fine-tuned-LLM benchmark — 292 spoken-form transcripts judged by both Claude Opus 4.7 and GPT-5.5 — and tested all four reasoning_effort levels: instant, low, medium, high.

The short version: Mercury 2 scales cleanly with reasoning effort, but even at the highest setting it lands below the unfine-tuned Qwen3 base models — and roughly 36 points behind a fine-tuned Qwen3.5-9B on the same eval.

Mercury 2 across reasoning levels

"Accuracy" is the share of rows the judge calls fully correct. Each row is graded independently by Opus 4.7 and GPT-5.5. Latency is single-call, one request at a time.

The curve is monotonic — every step up in reasoning effort adds roughly 7–10 percentage points of accuracy and roughly doubles latency. Catastrophic failures drop by more than half from instant to high. The two judges agree directionally; GPT-5.5 is consistently 10–13 points more lenient than Opus, the same pattern we see on the fine-tuned models.

One wrinkle: at high, Mercury 2's content filter blocked 6 of the 55 adversarial-prompt rows outright (HTTP 400 content_filter_error). We counted those as failures — they don't appear at lower reasoning levels.

Two ways to read this:

  • Mercury 2 at its best setting (high, 56%) sits between the unfine-tuned Qwen3-8B (60%) and the fine-tuned Gemma-3-1B (72%). It's 36 points behind the leading fine-tune.

The big stories in the heatmap:

  • NO_ENTITY is a complete miss. 0 / 30 at every reasoning level. These are conversational rows with no entities at all — the model should return the input nearly unchanged. Mercury 2 consistently restructures or "improves" them in a way the judge calls wrong.
  • Phone numbers, credit cards, and numeric IDs scale steeply with reasoning. Phone climbs 4 → 25 (out of 32), credit cards 3 → 18. These are tasks where the model genuinely benefits from "thinking" about digit grouping.
  • ADVERSARIAL degrades at high. 16 → 11 from instant → high, and catastrophic count jumps from 4 to 12. More reasoning means more chances to be tricked.
  • Dates do nearly perfectly at every level.

The reasoning-tokens cost

Mercury 2 bills reasoning tokens like output tokens. At high, the median row spends 517 reasoning tokens before emitting an answer — and the p90 is 1,162. For a workload that has to run on every transcript, that's a real cost line.

For perspective, the fine-tuned 9B model emits roughly 30–40 output tokens per transcript and uses no reasoning tokens at all. Mercury 2 at high uses about 15× more tokens per call to land 36 points behind on accuracy.

What this means

For this task — narrow, well-defined, with a clean training signal — a fine-tuned small transformer dominates. Mercury 2 is impressively fast at instant (167 ms p50) and improves cleanly with more reasoning, but neither speed nor reasoning is enough to close the gap against a 9B model that has actually seen the task at training time.

Where Mercury 2 might still be the right choice:

  • Tasks too broad to fine-tune for. The whole appeal of a reasoning model is generalization to problems you can't curate a training set for. Transcript formatting isn't one of them.
  • Cold-start scenarios. If you don't have training data yet, Mercury 2 at medium (49%) beats every unfine-tuned Qwen / Gemma / Llama base we tested, with serious latency advantages over the 9Bs.
  • Latency-critical paths with weak accuracy needs. 167 ms p50 is genuinely impressive — but the 33% accuracy at that setting rules it out for transcript formatting specifically.
